Since 1 May 2026, Section 8 of the Housing Act 1988 is the only legal route for a landlord in England to regain possession of a rented property without the tenant voluntarily agreeing to leave. With Section 21 no longer available, getting the Section 8 process right — every step, every detail — is essential. A single error on the notice form, a miscalculated notice period, or a poorly chosen method of service can invalidate the entire process and force you to start again from scratch.

This guide provides a detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of how to serve a Section 8 notice correctly under the current law as of 2026.

> **Disclaimer:** This guide is for information only and does not constitute legal advice. Always consult a solicitor for your specific situation.

## What Is a Section 8 Notice?

A Section 8 notice — formally called a "Notice Seeking Possession of a Property Let on an Assured Tenancy" — is the prescribed legal document that starts the process of ending a tenancy. It tells the tenant that you intend to apply to court for a possession order and explains the legal grounds on which you are relying.

The notice itself does not end the tenancy. It is the first step in a formal legal process. If the tenant does not leave voluntarily after the notice period expires, you must apply to the county court for a possession order. If the tenant still does not leave after a court order is made, you must apply for a warrant of possession to have bailiffs carry out the eviction. At no point can you change the locks, remove the tenant's belongings, or otherwise take matters into your own hands — doing so is a criminal offence.

## When to Use a Section 8 Notice

You should serve a Section 8 notice when you have a legitimate ground for possession and the situation cannot be resolved by other means. Common scenarios include:

- The tenant has accumulated significant rent arrears (Ground 8 for two months or more, Ground 10 for any arrears)
- The tenant persistently pays rent late (Ground 11)
- The tenant has breached a term of the tenancy agreement (Ground 12)
- The tenant has caused the property to deteriorate through neglect or deliberate damage (Ground 13)
- The tenant has engaged in antisocial behaviour or caused nuisance (Ground 14)
- You want to sell the property (Ground 1A — new under RRA 2025)
- You want to move back into the property (Ground 1)
- Your mortgage lender requires possession (Ground 2)

In many cases, it is worth attempting to resolve the issue directly with the tenant first — through a written letter, a meeting, or formal mediation. Courts take a dim view of landlords who jump straight to possession proceedings without first trying to address the problem. However, for serious arrears, violence, or criminal activity, immediate action is appropriate.

## Step-by-Step: How to Serve a Section 8 Notice

### Step 1 — Identify the Correct Ground or Grounds

Review the full list of Section 8 grounds in Schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1988 (as amended by the Renters' Rights Act 2025) and identify every ground that applies to your situation. You can — and should — cite multiple grounds on a single notice where possible.

**Why citing multiple grounds matters:** If you cite only Ground 8 (serious rent arrears — mandatory) and the tenant makes a partial payment before the hearing that reduces the arrears below two months, the ground fails and your entire claim is dismissed. But if you also cited Ground 10 (some rent arrears — discretionary) and Ground 11 (persistent late payment — discretionary), you still have a basis for your claim.

**Important restriction:** Grounds 1 and 1A (landlord moving in or selling) cannot be used within the first twelve months of the tenancy. If the tenancy started less than twelve months ago, these grounds are not available to you regardless of your circumstances.

### Step 2 — Gather Your Evidence Before Serving

Do not serve the notice until you have assembled the evidence you will need if the case reaches court. Serving the notice starts a clock — the notice period will expire, and you want to be ready to file your court application promptly.

**For rent arrears (Grounds 8, 10, 11):**

- A complete rent ledger showing every payment due and received from the start of the tenancy
- Bank statements confirming non-receipt of rent on the relevant dates
- Copies of any written communication about arrears — demand letters, emails, text messages
- If you have accepted partial payments, a record showing exactly how each was allocated

**For property damage or deterioration (Ground 13):**

- The check-in report and photographs from the start of the tenancy
- Subsequent inspection reports with dated photographs showing the deterioration
- Repair quotes or invoices from qualified contractors
- Any correspondence with the tenant about the damage

**For antisocial behaviour (Ground 14):**

- A chronological incident log with dates, times, and descriptions
- Written statements from affected neighbours
- Police reports and crime reference numbers
- Records from the local council's environmental health or antisocial behaviour team

**For breach of tenancy (Ground 12):**

- The relevant clause of the tenancy agreement
- Evidence of the breach (photographs, witness statements, correspondence)
- Written warnings sent to the tenant about the breach

**For selling or moving in (Grounds 1, 1A):**

- Evidence of genuine intent — estate agent instructions, property valuation, solicitor engagement (Ground 1A)
- Evidence you previously occupied the property as your home, or that you require it for occupation (Ground 1)
- Written notice given to the tenant at the start of the tenancy that you may seek possession on this ground

### Step 3 — Complete the Prescribed Form

You must use **Form 3** — the prescribed form for a Section 8 notice under the Housing Act 1988. Using any other format will invalidate the notice. The form is available from the GOV.UK website.

**What the form requires:**

- The full name and address of the tenant(s)
- The address of the property
- Which ground(s) you are relying on — you must tick the correct box(es) and include the full statutory text of each ground
- **Particulars** for each ground — this is where you explain the specific facts. For rent arrears, state the exact amount owed and the dates it relates to. For antisocial behaviour, describe the incidents. For selling, state your intention and the steps you have taken
- The **earliest date** on which you will begin court proceedings — this must be after the notice period has expired

**Writing effective particulars:** Be specific, factual, and concise. Do not use emotional language or make allegations you cannot support with evidence. For example:

- Good: "Monthly rent of £1,200 is payable on the 1st of each month. No payment was received for January 2026 or February 2026. Total arrears as at the date of this notice: £2,400."
- Bad: "The tenant has never bothered paying rent properly and owes a fortune."

### Step 4 — Calculate the Correct Notice Period

Each ground has its own notice period. If you cite multiple grounds with different notice periods, use the longest one. Getting the notice period wrong invalidates the notice.

| Ground | Notice Period |
|---|---|
| Ground 1 (landlord moving in) | 2 months |
| Ground 1A (selling the property) | 2 months |
| Ground 2 (mortgage lender) | 2 months |
| Ground 6 (redevelopment) | 2 months |
| Ground 8 (serious rent arrears) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 10 (some rent arrears) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 11 (persistent late payment) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 12 (breach of tenancy) | 2 months |
| Ground 13 (property deterioration) | 2 months |
| Ground 14 (serious antisocial behaviour) | Immediate to 2 weeks |
| Ground 14 (general nuisance) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 17 (false information) | 2 months |

The "earliest date for proceedings" on the form must be at least the notice period after the date of service. If you serve by post, add two working days for deemed delivery.

### Step 5 — Serve the Notice on the Tenant

The notice must be served on the tenant. If there are joint tenants, serve a copy on each one. Acceptable methods of service are:

**In person:** Hand the notice directly to the tenant. This is the most reliable method. Bring a witness who can later provide a statement confirming the date, time, and location of service. If the tenant refuses to take the document, place it at their feet or push it under the door — a witness should confirm this.

**First class post:** Send the notice by first class post to the property address. Under Section 7 of the Interpretation Act 1978, service by post is deemed to take effect on the second working day after posting. Keep the certificate of posting from the Post Office.

**Email:** Only if the tenancy agreement explicitly provides for service of notices by email. If the tenancy agreement is silent on this point, email service is not valid.

**Methods that are NOT valid:** Hand delivery to a family member or flatmate (unless they are a joint tenant). Text message. WhatsApp. Social media. Leaving the notice on the doorstep without pushing it through the letterbox. Pinning it to the front door.

### Step 6 — Keep Proof of Service

Courts will ask you to prove that the notice was served correctly. Keep the following records:

- **In person:** A signed witness statement recording the date, time, and location. A photograph of the served notice if appropriate
- **By post:** The Post Office certificate of posting. A photograph or copy of the envelope addressed to the tenant. A note of the date posted
- **By email:** A sent receipt or screenshot showing the date, time, recipient address, and the notice attached

### Step 7 — Wait for the Notice Period to Expire

You cannot apply to court until the full notice period has elapsed from the date of service. During this period, continue to document everything — additional arrears, further incidents, ongoing breaches. This evidence will strengthen your case at the hearing.

If the tenant contacts you to discuss the situation during the notice period, document the conversation but do not withdraw or amend the notice. You can still negotiate a voluntary departure, agree a repayment plan, or resolve the issue without going to court — but keep the notice in place as a backstop.

### Step 8 — Apply to the County Court

If the tenant has not vacated by the time the notice period expires, apply for a possession order. You can use the **Possession Claims Online** (PCOL) service through HMCTS, or file paper forms at your local county court.

**What you need to submit:**

- Completed court application form (Form N5 for possession)
- A copy of the Section 8 notice
- Proof of service
- A copy of the tenancy agreement
- Your supporting evidence bundle
- The court fee (currently £355)

### Step 9 — Attend the Possession Hearing

The court will list a hearing, typically four to eight weeks after your application. At the hearing, a district judge will review the evidence and hear from both parties.

For **mandatory grounds** (Grounds 1, 1A, 2, 6, 8), the judge checks whether the ground is made out on the evidence. If it is, the judge must grant a possession order — there is no discretion.

For **discretionary grounds** (Grounds 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 17), the judge also considers whether it is reasonable to grant possession, taking into account the tenant's circumstances and any steps they have taken to remedy the situation.

### Step 10 — Enforce If Necessary

If the court grants a possession order and the tenant does not leave by the specified date (usually 14 days, or up to 42 days in exceptional circumstances), apply for a **warrant of possession** (Form N325, fee £130). County court bailiffs will then attend the property on a scheduled date to enforce the eviction. You must never attempt to evict a tenant yourself.

## Common Mistakes That Invalidate a Section 8 Notice

**Using the wrong form.** The notice must be on the prescribed Form 3. A letter, even one that cites the correct grounds and provides all the required information, is not valid.

**Miscalculating the notice period.** If you give four weeks' notice when the ground requires two months, the notice is invalid. Always check the current notice period for each ground cited and use the longest one.

**Failing to include proper particulars.** Stating "the tenant owes rent" without specifying the amount, dates, and calculation is insufficient. Particulars must be specific enough that the tenant can understand exactly what is alleged.

**Serving on the wrong person.** The notice must be served on every tenant named in the tenancy agreement. If there are joint tenants and you serve on only one, the notice may be defective.

**Using an invalid service method.** Posting to an address other than the property (unless the tenant has provided a correspondence address), texting, or sending via social media will not constitute valid service.

**Citing a ground that is not available.** Attempting to use Ground 1A within the first twelve months of the tenancy, or Ground 1 when you never gave the required prior notice, will invalidate the notice for those grounds.

**Applying to court before the notice expires.** Even if it's one day early, the court will not accept your application.

## After the Notice Is Served: What to Expect

Once the notice is served, the tenant may respond in several ways:

- **Vacate voluntarily** — the best outcome. Confirm the departure in writing and conduct a check-out inspection
- **Pay off the arrears** — if this reduces arrears below the threshold for Ground 8, the mandatory ground falls but discretionary grounds may still apply
- **Contact you to negotiate** — be open to reasonable proposals but keep the notice in force
- **Ignore the notice** — this is common and simply means you proceed with the court application
- **Seek legal advice and challenge the notice** — the tenant may instruct a solicitor or contact a housing charity. Be prepared for your evidence to be scrutinised

Throughout this period, continue to document everything. Every additional missed rent payment, every further incident, every communication strengthens your position at the hearing.
