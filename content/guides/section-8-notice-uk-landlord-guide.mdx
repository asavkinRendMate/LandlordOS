With Section 21 'no-fault' evictions abolished from 1 May 2026 under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, Section 8 is now the **only route** for landlords in England to regain possession of their property without the tenant's agreement. Whether you're dealing with persistent rent arrears, property damage, or antisocial behaviour, getting the Section 8 process right is critical — mistakes can cost you months and thousands of pounds in legal fees.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know: the grounds you can rely on, the notice periods that apply after the RRA 2025 changes, a step-by-step serving process, and the evidence you'll need to succeed at court.

> **Disclaimer:** This guide is for information only and does not constitute legal advice. Always consult a solicitor for your specific situation.

## What Is a Section 8 Notice?

A Section 8 notice — formally a "Notice Seeking Possession" — is the legal mechanism under the **Housing Act 1988** (as amended) that allows a landlord to begin the process of regaining possession of a rented property. Unlike the now-abolished Section 21, a Section 8 notice requires you to cite **specific grounds** explaining why you need the property back.

The notice itself doesn't end the tenancy. It's the first step in a process that may ultimately require a court order if the tenant doesn't leave voluntarily.

**When you can use Section 8:**

- The tenant has breached their tenancy agreement (rent arrears, property damage, antisocial behaviour)
- You need the property back for a legitimate reason recognised by law (selling, moving back in)
- You have evidence to support at least one of the statutory grounds for possession

**Key difference from the old Section 21:**

Section 21 required no reason at all — you simply served notice and waited. Section 8 demands that you prove your case. The burden is on you as the landlord to demonstrate that a specific ground applies, and in many cases, you'll need to convince a judge.

## The Grounds for Possession

The Housing Act 1988 (Schedule 2) sets out numbered grounds for possession, split into two categories: **mandatory** and **discretionary**. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 has amended several of these and introduced new grounds.

### Mandatory Grounds — Court Must Grant Possession

If you prove a mandatory ground is met, the court **has no discretion** — it must make a possession order in your favour.

**Ground 1 — Landlord's Former or Future Home**

You previously occupied the property as your home and want to move back in, or you acquired the property with the intention of living in it. Under the RRA 2025, you must have given written notice to the tenant at the start of the tenancy that you may seek possession on this ground. You cannot use this ground within the first 12 months of the tenancy.

**Ground 1A — Landlord Selling the Property (New under RRA 2025)**

This is an entirely new ground introduced by the Renters' Rights Act 2025. You intend to sell the property with vacant possession. You cannot use this ground within the first 12 months of the tenancy, and you must provide evidence of a genuine intention to sell (e.g. estate agent instructions, valuation). If you re-let the property within 12 months of gaining possession, you may face penalties.

**Ground 8 — Serious Rent Arrears**

The tenant owes at least **two months' rent** (for monthly tenancies) at both the date the notice is served **and** the date of the court hearing. This is the most commonly used mandatory ground. If the arrears dip below two months at any point before the hearing, the ground fails — though you may still succeed on discretionary grounds 10 or 11.

### Discretionary Grounds — Court May Grant Possession

With discretionary grounds, the court considers whether it is **reasonable** to make a possession order, weighing both the landlord's and tenant's circumstances.

**Ground 10 — Some Rent Arrears**

Rent is unpaid at the date of service and the date of the hearing, but the arrears are less than two months. The court will consider whether the arrears are likely to be cleared and the tenant's payment history.

**Ground 11 — Persistent Late Payment**

The tenant has a pattern of paying rent late, even if they're not currently in arrears. You'll need a clear record showing repeated late payments over several months.

**Ground 12 — Breach of Tenancy Terms**

The tenant has broken a term of the tenancy agreement other than rent payment — for example, subletting without permission, keeping pets in breach of a no-pets clause, or carrying out unauthorised alterations.

**Ground 13 — Deterioration of Property**

The condition of the property or common areas has deteriorated due to the tenant's acts, neglect, or default (or that of anyone living with them). You'll need photographic evidence and ideally a dated inspection report.

**Ground 14 — Nuisance or Antisocial Behaviour**

The tenant, a member of their household, or a visitor has caused nuisance or annoyance to neighbours, or has been convicted of an offence committed at or near the property. Under the RRA 2025, this ground has been strengthened with clearer definitions of antisocial behaviour and an expedited process for the most serious cases.

**Ground 17 — False Information**

The tenant obtained the tenancy by providing false information — for example, fake references, forged payslips, or misrepresenting their identity.

### RRA 2025 Changes to Grounds

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 has made several important changes:

- **New Ground 1A** for landlord sale — filling the gap left by Section 21's abolition
- **Strengthened antisocial behaviour grounds** — clearer definitions and faster court processing for serious cases
- **Repeated rent arrears ground** — courts can now consider a pattern of falling into arrears and clearing them before hearings (addressing the "Ground 8 gaming" problem)
- **12-month protection** — Grounds 1, 1A, and certain other landlord-need grounds cannot be used within the first 12 months of a tenancy
- **Re-letting restrictions** — if you regain possession under Ground 1 or 1A and re-let within 12 months, you face financial penalties

## Notice Periods (Post-RRA 2025)

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 changed several notice periods. These are the current requirements:

| Ground | Notice Period |
|---|---|
| Ground 1 (landlord moving back in) | 2 months |
| Ground 1A (selling the property) | 2 months |
| Ground 8 (serious rent arrears) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 10 (some rent arrears) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 11 (persistent late payment) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 12 (breach of tenancy) | 2 months |
| Ground 13 (property deterioration) | 2 months |
| Ground 14 (serious antisocial behaviour) | Immediate to 2 weeks |
| Ground 14 (general nuisance) | 4 weeks |
| Ground 17 (false information) | 2 months |

> **Important:** These notice periods replaced the previous Housing Act 1988 periods from the date the RRA 2025 came into force. If you're relying on older guidance or template forms, check that they reflect the current periods — using the wrong notice period will invalidate your notice.

## Step-by-Step: How to Serve a Section 8 Notice

### Step 1 — Identify the Correct Grounds

Review the full list of grounds and select every ground that applies to your situation. You can cite multiple grounds on a single notice — and you should, where possible. For example, if a tenant has two months' arrears, cite both Ground 8 (mandatory) and Ground 10 (discretionary) as a fallback in case the arrears dip below two months before the hearing.

### Step 2 — Gather Your Evidence

Before serving the notice, compile the evidence you'll need if the case reaches court:

- **Rent arrears:** Rent ledger showing every payment due, received, and missed. Bank statements corroborating non-payment. Any written communication about the arrears
- **Property damage:** Inspection reports with dated, time-stamped photographs. The original check-in report showing the property's condition at the start of the tenancy. Repair quotes or invoices
- **Antisocial behaviour:** A written log of incidents with dates, times, and descriptions. Statements from neighbours. Police reports or crime reference numbers. Council noise team records
- **Breach of tenancy:** Copy of the relevant tenancy clause. Evidence of the breach (photographs, witness statements, correspondence)

### Step 3 — Complete the Prescribed Form

You must use **Form 3** — the prescribed form for a Section 8 notice under the Housing Act 1988. This is available from the GOV.UK website. The form requires you to:

- Specify which ground(s) you are relying on
- Provide the full text of each ground cited
- Give particulars explaining how the ground applies (e.g. "Rent of £1,200 was due on the 1st of each month. No payment has been received for January 2026 or February 2026. Total arrears as at the date of this notice: £2,400.")
- State the earliest date on which possession proceedings can begin (i.e. after the notice period expires)

### Step 4 — Serve the Notice Correctly

The notice must be served on the tenant. Acceptable methods of service:

- **In person** — hand it directly to the tenant. Ideally have a witness present
- **First class post** — to the property address. Allow two working days for deemed delivery
- **Email** — only if the tenancy agreement explicitly permits service of notices by email

Keep proof of service: a witness statement, a photo of the posted letter, or a delivery/read receipt for email. Courts will ask for this.

### Step 5 — Wait for the Notice Period to Expire

You cannot apply to court until the notice period has fully elapsed. Use the table above to determine the correct period for your ground(s). If you've cited multiple grounds with different notice periods, wait for the longest one.

### Step 6 — Apply to Court

If the tenant hasn't vacated by the end of the notice period, apply for a possession order through the county court. You can do this online via the **HMCTS Possession Claims Online** service. You'll need to submit:

- A copy of the Section 8 notice
- Proof of service
- The tenancy agreement
- Your supporting evidence
- The court fee (currently £355 for standard possession claims)

### Step 7 — Attend the Possession Hearing

A hearing will be listed, typically 4–8 weeks after your application. At the hearing:

- For **mandatory grounds**, the judge checks whether the ground is made out. If it is, possession must be granted — the judge has no discretion
- For **discretionary grounds**, the judge considers whether it's reasonable to grant possession, weighing your evidence against the tenant's circumstances (e.g. illness, children, alternative housing)

Bring all original evidence. Organise it clearly — judges appreciate chronological bundles with an index.

### Step 8 — Enforce If Necessary

If the court grants a possession order and the tenant still doesn't leave by the specified date, you'll need to apply for a **warrant of possession** (Form N325, fee £130). County court bailiffs will then attend the property to enforce the eviction. You must **never** attempt to evict a tenant yourself — this is a criminal offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977.

## Evidence: What Courts Actually Need

The single biggest reason landlords lose possession hearings is **insufficient evidence**. Courts need clear, dated, contemporaneous records — not a narrative assembled after the fact.

### For Rent Arrears (Grounds 8 and 10)

- A complete rent ledger from the start of the tenancy
- Bank statements showing non-receipt of rent
- Copies of any arrears letters or emails sent to the tenant
- If you accepted partial payments, a clear record showing how each payment was allocated

### For Property Damage (Ground 13)

- A check-in report with dated photographs showing the property's condition at the start
- Subsequent inspection reports showing deterioration
- Photographs with EXIF timestamps or embedded dates
- Repair quotes from contractors

### For Antisocial Behaviour (Ground 14)

- A chronological incident log (date, time, what happened, who witnessed it)
- Written statements from neighbours
- Police reports and crime reference numbers
- Environmental health or council records

### For Breach of Tenancy (Ground 12)

- The tenancy agreement clause that's been breached
- Written warnings sent to the tenant
- Evidence of the ongoing breach

### Why Inspection Reports Matter

A professional check-in report with dated, dual-signed photographs is one of the strongest pieces of evidence a landlord can have. When both landlord and tenant have acknowledged the condition of the property at a specific date, it becomes very difficult for a tenant to dispute damage claims later.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Using the wrong form.** The Section 8 notice must be on the prescribed Form 3. A letter or email citing the grounds is not sufficient and the court will dismiss your claim.

**Citing the wrong ground.** If you cite Ground 8 but the arrears are less than two months, the mandatory ground fails. Always include discretionary fallback grounds where applicable.

**Insufficient notice period.** Each ground has its own notice period. If you give 2 weeks when the ground requires 4 weeks, the notice is invalid and you'll have to start again.

**Poor evidence at the hearing.** Verbal assertions won't succeed. Judges expect documentary evidence — ideally an indexed, paginated bundle.

**Serving the notice incorrectly.** If you can't prove the tenant received the notice, the court may refuse to proceed. Always keep evidence of service.

**Not keeping the arrears above the threshold.** For Ground 8, the tenant must owe at least two months' rent both when you serve the notice and at the hearing. If they make a partial payment reducing the arrears below two months, the mandatory ground fails.

**Waiting too long to act.** Delays can undermine your case. If rent goes unpaid for months before you serve notice, the court may question why you waited. Act promptly and document everything from the start.

## What Happens at Court

### The Possession Hearing

Hearings are typically short — 15 to 30 minutes. You'll be before a district judge in a county court. Both you and the tenant (or your representatives) will have the opportunity to present your case.

For **mandatory grounds**, the hearing is relatively straightforward. The judge verifies that the ground is satisfied (e.g. checking that arrears are at least two months at the hearing date). If it is, the judge must make a possession order, usually giving the tenant 14 days to vacate.

For **discretionary grounds**, the judge has more scope. They'll consider:

- The strength of your evidence
- The tenant's circumstances (health, dependants, ability to find alternative housing)
- Whether the tenant has taken steps to remedy the breach
- The overall reasonableness of granting possession

### Possible Outcomes

- **Outright possession order** — the tenant must leave by a specified date (usually 14 days, extended to 42 days in exceptional circumstances)
- **Suspended possession order** — the order is made but suspended on conditions (e.g. the tenant pays current rent plus a set amount towards arrears each month). If the tenant breaches the conditions, you can apply to enforce without a new hearing
- **Dismissal** — the judge is not satisfied that the ground is made out, or finds it unreasonable to grant possession
- **Adjournment** — the case is postponed, usually to allow the tenant to get legal advice or provide further information

### Costs

- Court application fee: **£355** (standard possession)
- Hearing fee: included in the application fee
- Warrant of possession (if needed): **£130**
- Solicitor costs: vary widely, but budget **£1,500–£3,000** for a straightforward case
- If you win, the court may order the tenant to pay your costs, but recovering them from a tenant in arrears is often difficult

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I use Section 8 if the tenant is only one month behind on rent?

Yes, but only on discretionary Ground 10 (some rent arrears) or Ground 11 (persistent late payment). The mandatory Ground 8 requires at least two full months' arrears. On discretionary grounds, the court will decide whether it's reasonable to grant possession.

### How long does the whole process take?

From serving the notice to bailiff enforcement, expect **3 to 6 months** in a straightforward case. Complex or contested cases can take longer. The notice period is 4 weeks to 2 months, court listing takes 4–8 weeks, and enforcement adds another 4–6 weeks if needed.

### Can a tenant stop a Section 8 eviction?

For mandatory grounds, the tenant's options are limited — if the ground is proven, the court must grant possession. For discretionary grounds, the tenant can argue that it's not reasonable to evict them. For Ground 8 specifically, the tenant can defeat the claim by reducing arrears below two months before the hearing.

### What if the tenant causes damage but pays rent on time?

Use Ground 13 (deterioration of property) — this is a discretionary ground, so you'll need strong evidence and the court must find it reasonable. Dated inspection reports with photographs and tenant acknowledgment are essential.

### Do I need a solicitor?

There's no legal requirement to use a solicitor, and many landlords handle straightforward Ground 8 claims themselves. However, for discretionary grounds, contested cases, or if you're unfamiliar with court procedures, professional legal advice is strongly recommended.

### Can I serve a Section 8 notice within the first year of a tenancy?

For most grounds relating to tenant fault (rent arrears, antisocial behaviour, breach of tenancy), yes — there is no minimum tenancy period. However, Grounds 1 and 1A (landlord moving back in or selling) **cannot** be used within the first 12 months.

### What happens if my Section 8 notice has an error?

If the notice contains a material error — wrong form, incorrect notice period, missing ground particulars — the court will likely dismiss your application and you'll need to start the process again from scratch. This is why accuracy matters: double-check every detail before serving.

### Can I serve a Section 8 notice by text message?

No. Acceptable methods are in person, by first class post, or by email only if the tenancy agreement specifically permits email service of notices. Text messages, WhatsApp, and social media are not valid methods of service.
