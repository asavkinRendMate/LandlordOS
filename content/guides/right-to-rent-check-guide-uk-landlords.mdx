Right to rent checks are one of your most important legal obligations as a UK landlord. Get them wrong, and you could face fines of up to £3,000 per tenant or even imprisonment. This comprehensive guide walks you through everything you need to know about conducting lawful right to rent checks.

## What Are Right to Rent Checks?

Right to rent checks are mandatory identity and immigration status verifications you must complete before allowing anyone to occupy your rental property. Introduced in 2014 and extended across England in 2016, these checks ensure only people legally entitled to rent property in the UK can do so.

**Key point**: You must check **every adult** (18 or over) who will live in the property, including joint tenants, guarantors who will occupy the property, and any adults moving in during the tenancy.

## Legal Requirements Under UK Law

The Immigration Act 2014 makes right to rent checks a legal requirement for all landlords in England. Here's what the law demands:

### Your Legal Obligations

- **Check before occupation**: Complete checks before anyone moves in or signs a tenancy agreement
- **Check all adults**: Verify every person aged 18 or over who will live in the property
- **Keep records**: Retain copies of documents and check records throughout the tenancy
- **Repeat checks**: Conduct follow-up checks when someone's leave to remain expires
- **Report suspicious documents**: Alert the Home Office if you suspect fraudulent documentation

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

The consequences of failing to conduct proper right to rent checks are severe:

- **Civil penalty**: Up to £3,000 per tenant for a first offence
- **Criminal prosecution**: For repeat offences or knowing non-compliance
- **Prison sentence**: Up to 5 years for serious breaches
- **Landlord licensing**: Potential refusal or revocation of licensing applications

## Who Needs Right to Rent Checks?

### People You Must Check

✅ **All tenants** named on the tenancy agreement  
✅ **Joint tenants** and their partners if living together  
✅ **Guarantors** who will occupy the property  
✅ **Adult children** of tenants (once they turn 18)  
✅ **New occupants** added during the tenancy  

### People You Don't Need to Check

❌ Visitors staying temporarily  
❌ Children under 18  
❌ Guarantors who won't live in the property  
❌ Tenants' guests (unless they become permanent residents)

## Step-by-Step Right to Rent Check Process

### Step 1: Request Documentation

Ask prospective tenants to provide acceptable right to rent documents **before** you arrange viewings or accept applications. This saves everyone time if they can't demonstrate their right to rent.

**Best practice**: Include right to rent requirements in your property listings and initial tenant communications.

### Step 2: Examine Original Documents

You must see and handle **original documents** in person. Photocopies, scanned images, or digital versions aren't acceptable for the initial check.

**Important**: The person must be physically present when you examine their documents, unless you're using the online right to rent service for certain visa holders.

### Step 3: Check Document Authenticity

Examine documents carefully for signs of forgery or tampering:

- **Feel the paper**: Genuine documents have specific textures and thickness
- **Check security features**: Look for watermarks, holograms, and raised text
- **Verify photos**: Ensure photos match the person and aren't tampered with
- **Check dates**: Confirm documents haven't expired
- **Examine text**: Look for spelling errors or unusual fonts

### Step 4: Make and Keep Copies

Take clear copies of all documents showing:
- **Full document**: Both sides if applicable
- **Readable text**: All details must be legible
- **Colour copies**: Where security features require it
- **Date of check**: Add the date you conducted the check

### Step 5: Complete Your Records

Document the check with:
- Date of the check
- Name of person checked
- Type of document examined
- Document reference numbers
- Your signature confirming you've seen originals

## List A vs List B Documents

The Home Office categorises acceptable documents into two lists based on the person's right to rent status.

### List A Documents (Indefinite Right to Rent)

These documents prove indefinite right to rent - **no follow-up checks needed**:

**British and Irish Citizens:**
- UK or Irish passport (current or expired within 2 years)
- UK birth certificate + official document with National Insurance number
- Certificate of registration/naturalisation + official document with National Insurance number

**Other Indefinite Leave:**
- Passport with indefinite leave to remain stamp
- Biometric residence permit showing indefinite leave
- Passport with "no time limit" stamp

### List B Documents (Time-Limited Right to Rent)

These documents prove time-limited right to rent - **follow-up checks required**:

**Current Permission:**
- Passport with valid visa or entry stamp
- Biometric residence permit with expiry date
- Immigration status document from Home Office
- Application registration card (Group 2)

**Asylum Seekers and Appeals:**
- Certificate of application for asylum
- Positive verification from Home Office employer checking service

## Online Right to Rent Service

For certain visa holders, you can use the government's online right to rent checking service instead of examining physical documents.

### When to Use Online Service

- The person has a biometric residence permit, card, or passport
- They've provided you with their "share code"
- You've verified their identity using their physical document first

### How the Online Service Works

1. **Get share code**: Tenant generates a code at gov.uk/prove-right-to-rent
2. **Enter details**: Input the share code and tenant's date of birth
3. **View results**: See their right to rent status and any restrictions
4. **Keep records**: Print or save the results as your check record

**Advantage**: Online checks automatically show when follow-up checks are due.

## Follow-Up Checks: When and How

If someone has time-limited right to rent (List B documents), you must conduct follow-up checks before their permission expires.

### Timing Follow-Up Checks

- **Before expiry**: Check just before their leave to remain expires
- **During tenancy**: Even if they've been good tenants
- **New applications**: If they've applied for extended permission

### Follow-Up Check Process

1. **Contact tenant**: Ask for updated documentation at least 28 days before expiry
2. **Arrange meeting**: Meet in person to examine new documents
3. **Check status**: Verify their new right to rent status
4. **Update records**: Keep copies of new documents and check records

### If They Can't Provide Valid Documents

If a tenant can't demonstrate ongoing right to rent:

- **Don't renew**: Don't offer tenancy renewals
- **Serve notice**: Serve appropriate notice to quit
- **Report to Home Office**: Notify immigration enforcement
- **Keep records**: Document your actions and communications

## Record Keeping Requirements

Proper record keeping protects you from penalties and provides evidence of compliance.

### What Records to Keep

**Document copies:**
- Clear, legible copies of all checked documents
- Both sides of documents where applicable
- Colour copies when security features require it

**Check records:**
- Date and location of each check
- Names of all people checked
- Type of documents examined
- Your signature and dated confirmation

**Correspondence:**
- Communications about right to rent requirements
- Tenant responses and any issues raised
- Follow-up check arrangements and outcomes

### How Long to Keep Records

- **During tenancy**: Keep all records while tenancy continues
- **After tenancy**: Retain records for at least **one year** after tenancy ends
- **Safe storage**: Keep physical and digital copies secure
- **Easy access**: Ensure records are quickly retrievable if requested

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Documentation Errors

❌ **Accepting photocopies**: Always see originals first  
❌ **Skipping verification**: Don't assume documents are genuine  
❌ **Missing follow-ups**: Set reminders for time-limited permissions  
❌ **Incomplete records**: Keep comprehensive check documentation  

### Process Mistakes

❌ **Checking after move-in**: Complete checks before occupation  
❌ **Missing occupants**: Check everyone aged 18 or over  
❌ **Ignoring expiry dates**: Monitor when permissions expire  
❌ **Poor record keeping**: Maintain organised, accessible records  

## Special Circumstances

### Joint Tenancies

When renting to joint tenants:
- Check each tenant individually
- Each must have right to rent
- One person's status doesn't affect others
- Keep separate records for each person

### Adding New Occupants

If someone new moves in during the tenancy:
- Conduct full right to rent check before they occupy
- Update tenancy agreement if necessary
- Add their details to your records
- Monitor any time-limited permissions

### Student Accommodation

For student lettings:
- Check all students aged 18 or over
- International students often have time-limited visas
- Plan follow-up checks around academic years
- Keep university contacts for visa verification

## Discrimination and Fair Treatment

Right to rent checks must be conducted fairly without discrimination.

### Legal Requirements

- **Check everyone equally**: Same process for all prospective tenants
- **Don't assume status**: Never guess someone's immigration status
- **Accept all valid documents**: Don't insist on specific document types
- **Reasonable adjustments**: Make accommodations for disabilities

### Avoiding Discrimination

**Do:**
- Request documents from all prospective tenants
- Accept any valid List A or List B document
- Treat all applications equally
- Focus on ability to pay rent and be good tenants

**Don't:**
- Make assumptions based on appearance or accent
- Ask for specific documents from certain groups
- Refuse applications based on immigration status alone
- Treat people differently during the checking process

## Staying Compliant: Best Practices

### Create a Standard Process

Develop a consistent approach to right to rent checks:

1. **Initial contact**: Explain requirements upfront
2. **Document request**: Ask for proof before viewings
3. **Physical check**: Always examine originals in person
4. **Record keeping**: Maintain comprehensive records
5. **Follow-up system**: Track and schedule repeat checks

### Regular Training and Updates

- **Stay informed**: Monitor changes to right to rent requirements
- **Update procedures**: Adjust processes when rules change
- **Train staff**: Ensure anyone conducting checks is properly trained
- **Review regularly**: Assess and improve your checking procedures

### Building Positive Relationships

Approach right to rent checks professionally:
- **Explain the process**: Help tenants understand legal requirements

- **Maintain privacy**: Keep personal information secure and confidential
