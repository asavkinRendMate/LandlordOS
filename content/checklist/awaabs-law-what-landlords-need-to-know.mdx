Awaab's Law is one of the most significant changes to housing safety regulations in recent years. Named after two-year-old Awaab Ishak, who died in December 2020 from a respiratory condition caused by prolonged exposure to mould in his family's social housing flat in Rochdale, the law sets strict timescales for landlords to investigate and fix damp, mould, and other serious hazards.

Originally introduced for social housing providers through the Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023, the Renters' Rights Act 2025 extends these requirements to private landlords. This guide explains what the law requires, the timescales you must meet, and the practical steps you can take to comply.

## Background: The Awaab Ishak Case

Awaab Ishak's family had reported mould in their flat to their housing association, Rochdale Boroughwide Housing, multiple times over a period of years. Despite repeated complaints, the mould was not adequately addressed. A coroner's inquest found that Awaab's death was caused by prolonged exposure to mould, and the housing association was criticised for failing to act.

The case drew national attention to the dangers of damp and mould in rented housing and the inadequacy of existing enforcement mechanisms. Parliament responded by introducing what became known as Awaab's Law, creating legally binding timescales for landlords to respond to hazardous conditions.

## What the Law Requires

Awaab's Law imposes specific, measurable timescales on landlords for dealing with reports of damp, mould, and other hazards that pose a risk to the health and safety of tenants. The law creates a duty to:

- **Acknowledge** tenant reports of hazards promptly
- **Investigate** reported hazards within a defined timescale
- **Begin remedial work** within a defined timescale after investigation
- **Complete emergency repairs** urgently when there is an immediate risk
- **Keep records** of all reports, investigations, and remedial actions

These are not guidelines or best practice recommendations. They are legally enforceable requirements with penalties for non-compliance.

## Which Hazards Are Covered

### Damp and Mould

The primary focus of Awaab's Law is damp and mould, which remain the most common serious hazards in UK rented housing. This includes:

- **Penetrating damp** caused by water entering through the building fabric
- **Rising damp** from ground moisture wicking up through walls
- **Condensation damp** caused by inadequate ventilation, insulation, or heating
- **Mould growth** of any type, whether black mould (Stachybotrys), green mould, or other varieties
- **Damp patches**, peeling wallpaper, and other visible signs of moisture problems

### Other Serious Hazards

Beyond damp and mould, the law covers other conditions that pose a serious risk to health and safety, including:

- **Structural defects** that could cause injury
- **Electrical hazards** such as faulty wiring or exposed cables
- **Gas safety issues** including carbon monoxide risks
- **Excess cold** from inadequate heating or insulation
- **Fire safety deficiencies** affecting escape routes or fire detection
- **Falls hazards** from broken stairs, missing handrails, or uneven floors
- **Water supply contamination** or drainage failures

The Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) provides the framework for assessing which hazards are sufficiently serious to fall within the scope of the law.

## Response Timescales

The law sets out clear timescales that landlords must meet from the point a tenant reports a hazard. These timescales are strict and do not allow for vague promises of action at some future date.

### Step 1: Acknowledge the Report

**Timescale: Within 14 calendar days of receiving the report**

When a tenant reports a potential hazard, you must:

- Acknowledge receipt of the report in writing
- Confirm what has been reported
- Explain the next steps (investigation)
- Provide a point of contact for the tenant

This acknowledgment must be a genuine response, not an automated reply. It should demonstrate that you have read and understood the tenant's concern.

### Step 2: Investigate the Hazard

**Timescale: Within 14 calendar days of the acknowledgment**

After acknowledging the report, you must investigate the hazard. This means:

- Arranging an inspection of the property (with appropriate notice to the tenant)
- Identifying the cause of the problem, not just the symptoms
- Assessing the severity of the hazard
- Determining what remedial work is needed
- Communicating your findings to the tenant

For damp and mould, this often means distinguishing between condensation caused by lifestyle factors (which may require tenant education) and structural issues (which require building work). Even if the cause is partly related to tenant behaviour, you still have an obligation to ensure the property's fabric, heating, and ventilation are adequate.

### Step 3: Begin Remedial Work

The timescale for beginning repairs depends on the severity of the hazard.

**Emergency hazards (immediate risk to health or safety): Begin work within 24 hours**

An emergency exists when there is an immediate danger to the tenant's health or safety. Examples include:

- A gas leak or suspected carbon monoxide exposure
- Complete heating failure in winter
- Structural collapse or imminent risk of collapse
- Electrical hazard with risk of electrocution
- Severe water ingress making the property uninhabitable

For emergencies, you must take action to make the property safe within 24 hours, even if the full repair will take longer.

**Non-emergency hazards: Begin work within 7 days of completing the investigation**

For hazards that are serious but do not pose an immediate danger, such as persistent damp patches or mould growth that has not yet reached dangerous levels, you must begin remedial work within 7 days of completing your investigation.

**Standard hazards: Begin work within 28 days of completing the investigation**

For hazards that require attention but are less urgent, such as minor damp in a non-habitable area or early-stage condensation issues, you must begin remedial work within 28 days.

### Completing the Work

The law expects remedial work to be completed within a reasonable timescale. While no single fixed deadline applies to all repairs (since the scope of work varies enormously), you must:

- Proceed without unnecessary delay once work has begun
- Keep the tenant informed of progress
- Complete the work to a standard that resolves the hazard
- Conduct a follow-up check to confirm the issue has been resolved

## When Awaab's Law Applies to Private Landlords

Awaab's Law was first introduced for registered social housing providers. The **Renters' Rights Act 2025** extends these obligations to private landlords in England, bringing the private rented sector in line with social housing.

### Key Points for Private Landlords

- The timescales apply to **all private residential tenancies** in England
- You are expected to have systems in place for tenants to report hazards
- Ignorance of a hazard does not excuse non-compliance if the tenant has reported it
- The requirements apply whether you manage the property yourself or use an agent
- If you use a managing agent, you remain legally responsible for compliance

### Transition Period

The government has provided guidance on the implementation timeline. If you are not already meeting these timescales, you should begin adapting your processes now. The legal obligations are in force, and enforcement action can be taken against landlords who fail to comply.

## Documentation Requirements

Proper record keeping is essential for demonstrating compliance with Awaab's Law. You should maintain records of:

### Tenant Reports

- Date and time the report was received
- Method of communication (email, phone, letter, in-person)
- Details of the hazard as described by the tenant
- Any photos or evidence provided by the tenant

### Your Response

- Date of acknowledgment
- Copy of the acknowledgment sent to the tenant
- Date of investigation/inspection
- Findings of the investigation
- Assessment of severity and urgency
- Communication of findings to the tenant

### Remedial Work

- Date work was instructed
- Details of contractors appointed
- Scope of work to be carried out
- Date work began
- Progress updates sent to the tenant
- Date of completion
- Follow-up inspection results
- Tenant confirmation that the issue is resolved

### Ongoing Monitoring

For recurring issues like damp and mould, you may need to document ongoing monitoring to demonstrate that the problem has been properly resolved and has not returned.

## Tenant Reporting Obligations

While the primary obligations fall on landlords, tenants also have responsibilities:

- **Report hazards promptly** rather than allowing them to worsen
- **Provide reasonable access** for inspections and remedial work
- **Cooperate with remedial measures**, such as allowing furniture to be moved
- **Follow any reasonable guidance** on ventilation, heating, and condensation management
- **Not deliberately cause or worsen** hazardous conditions

However, a tenant's failure to report a hazard does not excuse a landlord who becomes aware of the issue through other means, such as a routine inspection.

## What Constitutes an Emergency

Understanding what qualifies as an emergency is critical, as it triggers the shortest response time (24 hours). An emergency under Awaab's Law is a situation where:

- There is an **immediate risk to the health or safety** of the tenant or their household
- The hazard could cause **serious harm** if not addressed urgently
- **Waiting for a standard response** would expose the tenant to unacceptable risk

### Examples of Emergencies

- Gas leak or carbon monoxide alarm activation
- Total heating failure when temperatures are below freezing
- Major water leak causing flooding or risk of electrical contact
- Structural movement making part of the property unsafe to occupy
- Severe mould in a child's bedroom causing respiratory distress
- Complete loss of hot water or sanitation

### Examples That Are Not Typically Emergencies

- Slow dripping tap
- Minor cosmetic damp patch in a hallway
- Slight condensation on windows in winter
- A cracked (but not broken) window pane
- A sticking door

The key test is whether delaying action would create a real and immediate risk of harm.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failure to comply with Awaab's Law timescales can result in:

### Local Authority Enforcement

- **Improvement notices** requiring you to carry out specific work within a set timescale
- **Prohibition orders** preventing all or part of the property from being used
- **Emergency prohibition orders** if there is an immediate risk to health
- **Civil penalties** of up to £30,000 per offence
- **Criminal prosecution** for serious or repeated breaches

### Rent Repayment Orders

Tenants may apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a **rent repayment order** if you have committed certain housing offences, potentially requiring you to repay up to 12 months' rent.

### Impact on Possession Proceedings

Failure to address hazards may affect your ability to seek possession of the property. Tribunals and courts will take your compliance record into account when considering possession applications.

## Practical Steps to Comply

### 1. Set Up a Clear Reporting Channel

Make it easy for tenants to report issues. Provide:

- An email address that you check regularly
- A phone number with a voicemail option
- Written confirmation of how to report urgent issues out of hours

### 2. Respond Promptly to Every Report

Even if a report seems minor, acknowledge it within the 14-day timescale. Many disputes arise not from the severity of the issue, but from a tenant feeling ignored.

### 3. Investigate Properly

Do not guess at the cause of damp or mould. Arrange a proper inspection, ideally by a qualified surveyor or specialist contractor who can identify the root cause.

### 4. Fix the Cause, Not Just the Symptoms

Painting over mould without addressing the underlying damp issue is not compliance. The law requires you to resolve the hazard, which means fixing the root cause.

### 5. Keep Thorough Records

Document everything. If enforcement action is ever taken, your records are your primary defence.

### 6. Conduct Preventive Maintenance

The best way to comply with Awaab's Law is to prevent hazards from arising in the first place:

- Ensure adequate ventilation in kitchens and bathrooms (extractor fans, trickle vents)
- Maintain gutters, downpipes, and external drainage
- Check roofing and flashings regularly
- Ensure the heating system works efficiently
- Address condensation issues proactively

### 7. Educate Your Tenants

Provide clear guidance on:

- How to ventilate the property properly
- The importance of using extractor fans when cooking or bathing
- Not drying clothes on radiators without opening windows
- Reporting issues early rather than waiting for them to worsen

## Conclusion

Awaab's Law represents a fundamental shift in the standards expected of landlords when it comes to property safety. The strict timescales may feel demanding, but they are designed to prevent the kind of tragedy that prompted the law in the first place. For most responsible landlords who already respond promptly to maintenance issues, the formal requirements will not require a dramatic change in behaviour.
