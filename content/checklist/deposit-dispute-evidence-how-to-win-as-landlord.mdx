Most landlords who go through a deposit dispute lose. Not because the tenant did nothing wrong, but because the landlord cannot prove what went wrong. Deposit adjudication is an evidence-based process, and the landlord bears the burden of proof. If your evidence is incomplete, poorly organised, or missing entirely, you will almost certainly lose — regardless of the actual damage to your property.

This guide explains exactly how the deposit dispute process works, what evidence you need, how to build your case from day one, and how to avoid the common mistakes that cost landlords thousands of pounds every year.

> **Disclaimer:** This guide is for information only and does not constitute legal advice.

## The Numbers: Why Landlords Lose

The statistics paint a clear picture. According to data published by the three government-approved deposit protection schemes (TDS, DPS, and MyDeposits), landlords receive the full amount they claimed in only a minority of disputed cases. In the majority of disputes, the adjudicator either awards the tenant the full deposit back or reduces the landlord's claim significantly.

The most common reason cited by adjudicators for ruling against landlords is **insufficient evidence**. Not that the landlord was lying or the tenant was blameless, but that the landlord simply could not demonstrate their case to the required standard.

This is fixable. Landlords who understand what evidence is needed and build their documentation from the first day of the tenancy win disputes at significantly higher rates.

## How the Adjudication Process Works

Understanding the process helps you prepare for it. Here is what happens step by step.

### Deposit protection basics

By law, landlords in England must protect tenancy deposits in one of three government-approved schemes within 30 days of receiving them:

- **Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)** — offers both custodial (free) and insured options
- **Deposit Protection Service (DPS)** — custodial scheme, free to use
- **MyDeposits** — offers both custodial and insured options

You must also provide the tenant with prescribed information about the scheme within the same 30-day period. Failure to protect the deposit or serve prescribed information can result in penalties of one to three times the deposit amount and prevent you from using certain grounds for possession.

### When a dispute arises

At the end of the tenancy, you have 10 working days (the exact period varies by scheme) to agree with the tenant on how the deposit should be divided. If you want to make deductions, you must notify the tenant in writing, specifying each deduction and the amount.

If the tenant disputes your proposed deductions, either party can refer the matter to the deposit protection scheme's free alternative dispute resolution (ADR) service. This is an adjudication process — not mediation. An independent adjudicator reviews the evidence submitted by both parties and makes a binding decision.

### The adjudication process

1. **The landlord submits evidence** supporting their proposed deductions — typically a written submission, check-in and check-out reports, photographs, invoices, and correspondence
2. **The tenant submits evidence** challenging the deductions — their version of events, any photographs they have, and their response to each claimed deduction
3. **The adjudicator reviews both submissions** — they do not visit the property, interview witnesses, or conduct their own investigation. They decide solely on the documents submitted
4. **A binding decision is issued** — the adjudicator decides how the deposit should be divided, and the scheme enforces the decision

The critical point: the adjudicator only sees what you submit. If your evidence is strong, your case is strong. If your evidence is weak or missing, you lose — no matter what actually happened.

### The burden of proof

In deposit disputes, the **landlord bears the burden of proof**. This means it is your responsibility to demonstrate, on the balance of probabilities, that:

1. The property was in a specific condition at the start of the tenancy
2. The property was in a worse condition at the end
3. The deterioration was caused by the tenant (not fair wear and tear)
4. The amount you are claiming is reasonable

If you cannot prove any one of these four elements for a particular deduction, the adjudicator will reject that part of your claim.

## What Constitutes Strong Evidence

### Move-in (check-in) report

This is the foundation of your entire case. Without a detailed, dated, signed move-in report, you cannot prove what condition the property was in when the tenant took possession.

A strong check-in report includes:
- Room-by-room written condition descriptions
- Dated, timestamped photographs of every room, fixture, and fitting
- Condition grading using a consistent system
- Meter readings with photographs
- Key and access device inventory
- Tenant signature confirming agreement with the assessment
- Professional presentation (not handwritten notes on scrap paper)

An adjudicator seeing a comprehensive, digitally signed check-in report with timestamped photographs knows immediately that you documented the property professionally. This sets the tone for your entire case.

### Move-out (check-out) report

The check-out report must mirror the check-in report in structure and detail. It should cover the same rooms, use the same grading system, and photograph the same areas from the same angles. This allows direct comparison.

A strong check-out report includes:
- The same room-by-room structure as the check-in
- Photographs from the same angles showing current condition
- Clear identification of deterioration or damage since check-in
- Meter readings
- Key return record
- Tenant signature where possible (tenants may refuse if they dispute the findings)

### Dated photographs

Photographs are your most powerful evidence. Adjudicators are visual — a clear photograph showing damage is worth far more than a paragraph of written description.

For photographs to carry maximum weight:
- They must be **dated** (EXIF metadata or visible date reference)
- They must be **unedited** (no cropping, filtering, or enhancement)
- They must be **clear and well-lit** (blurry or dark photos are worthless)
- They should include **scale reference** (a coin or ruler next to damage)
- They should show **both context and detail** (wide shot of the room, then close-up of the damage)

### Invoices and receipts

If you are claiming the cost of repairs or cleaning, you need evidence of those costs. Adjudicators expect:
- **Actual invoices** from contractors, not estimates
- **Receipts** for materials purchased
- **Quotes** from at least two sources if the amount is significant
- **Evidence that costs are reasonable** — if a professional clean costs the average market rate, it is reasonable. If it costs three times the market rate, the adjudicator will question it

### Correspondence

Every communication with the tenant about property condition is evidence. Keep records of:
- Written notifications about damage discovered during the tenancy
- Maintenance requests and your responses
- Periodic inspection communications
- End-of-tenancy negotiation correspondence
- Any admissions by the tenant that damage occurred

Email and messaging records with dates are ideal. Verbal conversations should be followed up in writing confirming what was discussed.

## Fair Wear and Tear vs Damage

This is where most deposit disputes are won or lost. Landlords cannot charge tenants for **fair wear and tear** — the natural deterioration that occurs through normal everyday use of the property.

### What constitutes fair wear and tear

The concept is not defined in statute, but adjudicators apply a common-sense standard based on:

- **The length of the tenancy** — a carpet showing wear after 5 years is fair; a carpet showing the same wear after 5 months is not
- **The age and quality of the item** — a 15-year-old carpet was already near the end of its life; a new carpet should last longer
- **The number of occupants** — higher occupancy means more wear
- **The type of property** — a family home wears differently from a single-occupancy flat

**Examples of fair wear and tear:**
- Slight scuffing of paintwork around light switches
- Minor indentations in carpet from furniture
- Fading of curtains due to sunlight
- Small nail holes from hanging pictures
- Slight wear on door handles and locks

**Examples of damage (not fair wear and tear):**
- Cigarette burns on carpet or furniture
- Large holes in walls
- Broken windows or door panels
- Heavy staining from spillages not cleaned up
- Missing or broken fixtures

### Calculating deductions fairly

When damage is identified, deductions must be **proportionate and evidenced**. Key principles:

**Betterment.** You cannot charge a tenant the full cost of replacing a 10-year-old carpet with a new one. You must account for the age and remaining useful life of the item. If a carpet had 5 years of useful life remaining and the tenant's damage has ended that life, you can claim 5 years' worth, not the full replacement cost.

**Like-for-like.** Replacements should be of equivalent quality, not an upgrade. If the original carpet was a basic grade, you cannot claim for a premium replacement.

**Reasonable costs.** Cleaning and repair costs must be at market rates. Adjudicators will reject inflated invoices.

## Timeline for Raising Disputes

Timing matters. If you miss the deadlines, you may lose your right to claim:

- **End of tenancy:** Inspect the property and identify any deductions needed
- **Within 10 working days** (varies by scheme): Notify the tenant in writing of proposed deductions with supporting evidence
- **Negotiation period:** Both parties attempt to agree. This typically runs for 15-20 working days depending on the scheme
- **If no agreement:** Either party raises a formal dispute with the deposit scheme
- **Evidence submission deadline:** Both parties submit evidence within the scheme's specified timeframe (usually 14 days from dispute being raised)
- **Adjudication:** Decision typically within 28 days of evidence submission

Do not delay. If you fail to propose deductions promptly, adjudicators may infer that the issues were not significant enough to warrant a claim.

## Common Mistakes That Lose Cases

### No check-in report

This is the single most common reason landlords lose. Without a baseline, you cannot prove the property was in better condition at the start than at the end. Adjudicators will not accept your verbal assertion that "the property was immaculate when they moved in."

### Poor-quality photographs

Dark, blurry, or undated photographs carry minimal evidential weight. If an adjudicator cannot clearly see what the photograph is supposed to show, it does not help your case.

### Claiming for fair wear and tear

Attempting to charge for normal deterioration undermines your credibility. Adjudicators who see excessive or unreasonable claims often become sceptical of the entire submission, including legitimate items.

### Inflated costs

Quoting premium prices for basic repairs or cleaning suggests you are trying to profit from the deposit rather than recover genuine losses. Always provide market-rate evidence.

### Not itemising deductions

A single lump sum claim ("I want to deduct GBP 500 for cleaning and repairs") will almost certainly fail. Each deduction must be separately identified, evidenced, and costed.

### Missing the deadline

If you do not propose deductions within the required timeframe, the scheme may return the full deposit to the tenant automatically.

### Failing to respond to tenant challenges

If the tenant disputes your deductions and you do not respond to their specific arguments with evidence, the adjudicator may accept the tenant's version by default.

## Building Your Evidence from Day One

The landlords who win deposit disputes do not start building their case at the end of the tenancy. They build it from the beginning.

### At the start of the tenancy

- Create a comprehensive, professionally presented move-in inspection report
- Photograph every room, fixture, fitting, and existing mark
- Get the tenant to sign off on the report
- Provide the tenant with a copy immediately
- Store the report and all photographs securely in at least two locations

### During the tenancy

- Conduct periodic inspections (every 3-6 months) with photographs
- Keep records of all maintenance requests and responses
- Document any tenant-reported issues and how they were resolved
- Maintain a correspondence log
- Photograph any concerns identified during inspections

### At the end of the tenancy

- Conduct the check-out inspection using the same format as the check-in
- Photograph the same areas from the same angles
- Compare check-in and check-out reports side by side
- Identify deterioration beyond fair wear and tear
- Obtain repair and cleaning quotes before proposing deductions
- Present deductions clearly, with evidence for each item

## Presenting Your Case Effectively

When you submit your evidence to the adjudicator, presentation matters. A well-organised submission demonstrates professionalism and makes the adjudicator's job easier — which works in your favour.

### Structure your submission clearly

- Start with a summary of your case and the deductions claimed
- Present each deduction separately with its own evidence
- For each deduction, include: the check-in evidence, the check-out evidence, photographs showing the comparison, and the cost evidence
- Include a schedule of deductions with amounts

### Keep it factual

Avoid emotional language, accusations, or irrelevant complaints about the tenant. Adjudicators want facts and evidence, not opinions about character.

### Be reasonable

If you are claiming for 10 items but 2 are clearly fair wear and tear, drop those 2. A focused, reasonable claim is more credible than an exhaustive one that includes marginal items. Adjudicators notice when landlords overclaim, and it colours their view of the entire submission.

## Conclusion

Winning deposit disputes is not about being lucky or having a sympathetic adjudicator. It is about evidence. Landlords who document their property thoroughly at the start, maintain records throughout the tenancy, and present their case clearly at the end will recover legitimate costs. Landlords who do not will lose, regardless of the actual damage.

The investment in proper documentation — a thorough move-in report, periodic inspections, organised records — is small compared to the cost of losing a deposit dispute. Start building your evidence from day one, and you will never find yourself on the wrong end of an adjudicator's decision.
