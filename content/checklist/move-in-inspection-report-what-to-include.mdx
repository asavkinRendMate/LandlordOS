A move-in inspection report is the single most important document you will create at the start of any tenancy. It records the exact condition of your property on the day the tenant takes possession, and it becomes your primary evidence if anything goes wrong later — from deposit disputes to court proceedings for property damage.

Despite its importance, many landlords treat the move-in inspection as a box-ticking exercise. They scribble a few notes, snap some hasty photos, and hand over the keys. Months or years later, when they need to prove the property was in good condition at the start, they find their evidence is too thin to stand up to scrutiny.

This guide explains exactly what to include, how to photograph everything properly, and why a thorough move-in inspection report matters far more than most landlords realise.

> **Disclaimer:** This guide is for information only and does not constitute legal advice.

## What Is a Move-In Inspection Report?

A move-in inspection report (sometimes called a check-in report or inventory report) is a detailed written and photographic record of a property's condition at the start of a tenancy. It documents the state of every room, fixture, fitting, and appliance — along with meter readings, key counts, and any pre-existing damage.

The report serves as the agreed baseline. When the tenant eventually moves out, you compare the move-out condition against the move-in report to determine whether any damage occurred during the tenancy and whether deposit deductions are justified.

### Is a move-in inspection legally required?

There is no law in England or Wales that explicitly requires landlords to produce a move-in inspection report. You will not face a fine or prosecution for failing to create one.

However, practical reality makes it essential. The three government-approved deposit protection schemes — TDS, DPS, and MyDeposits — all state clearly that landlords must provide **evidence** to justify any deposit deductions. Without a dated, detailed record of the property's condition at the start, you have no baseline to prove that damage occurred during the tenancy rather than before it.

The result is straightforward: landlords without proper move-in reports lose the vast majority of deposit disputes. Not because they are wrong about the damage, but because they cannot prove their case.

## What to Include in Your Report

A comprehensive move-in inspection report covers every part of the property systematically. Rushing through or skipping sections creates gaps that tenants and adjudicators will exploit later.

### Property details and tenancy information

Start with the basics. These identify the report and link it to a specific tenancy:

- Full property address including postcode
- Date and time of the inspection
- Names of all persons present (landlord, tenant, agent, witness)
- Tenancy start date
- Tenancy agreement reference (if applicable)
- Weather conditions at the time of inspection (relevant for exterior assessments)

### Meter readings

Record gas, electricity, and water meter readings with photographs of each meter showing the reading clearly. This protects both parties from billing disputes and establishes responsibility for utility costs from the precise moment of handover.

Note the meter serial numbers as well as the readings. If there are prepayment meters, record the credit balance. If there are smart meters, note the display readings and whether they are in credit or prepayment mode.

### Keys and access devices

Document every key and access device handed over:

- Number and type of door keys (front, back, side)
- Window keys
- Garage or shed keys
- Fob or entry system codes for communal areas
- Mailbox keys
- Alarm codes and fobs

Record the total count and get the tenant to confirm receipt in writing. Lost keys are one of the most common — and most easily avoidable — deposit deduction disputes.

### Room-by-room condition assessment

Work through every room in the property, including hallways, landings, staircases, cupboards, utility areas, and any outdoor spaces. For each room, record the condition of:

**Walls and ceilings** — Note the decoration type (painted, wallpapered, tiled), colour, and condition. Record any marks, cracks, scuffs, damp patches, or stains. Specify locations precisely ("2cm scuff mark on north wall, 1m from floor, behind where door handle meets wall").

**Flooring** — Note the type (carpet, laminate, vinyl, tiles, hardwood) and condition. Record stains, wear patterns, loose areas, chips, or damage. For carpets, note whether they are professionally cleaned.

**Windows and window furniture** — Check that every window opens, closes, and locks properly. Note the condition of glass, frames, sills, handles, and locks. Record any condensation, cracks, or failed double glazing.

**Doors and door furniture** — Check every internal and external door. Note the condition of the door itself, hinges, handles, locks, and frames. Record sticking, squeaking, damage, or missing furniture.

**Light fittings and electrical outlets** — Note the type and condition of all light fittings. Check that all switches and sockets work. Record any missing bulbs, cracked faceplates, or loose fittings.

**Radiators and heating controls** — Check each radiator works and note its condition. Record the thermostat settings and check the programmer functions correctly.

**Furniture and furnishings** — If the property is furnished or part-furnished, list and describe every item. Note condition, brand, and any existing damage. This is critical because furniture disputes are common and expensive.

### Kitchen specifics

Kitchens require particular attention because they contain the most fixtures and appliances:

- Worktop surfaces — material, condition, burns, stains, chips
- Sink and taps — working condition, draining speed, any leaks
- Splashback and tiling — condition, grout state, any cracks
- Cupboard and drawer units — all doors opening and closing, hinges, handles
- Oven and hob — working condition, cleanliness, all burners/elements functioning
- Extractor fan — working condition, filter cleanliness
- Fridge and freezer — working condition, temperature, cleanliness, ice build-up
- Washing machine and dryer — working condition, connections, drum cleanliness
- Dishwasher — working condition, filter condition

For each appliance, record the make, model, and approximate age. Test everything while the tenant is present.

### Bathroom specifics

- Bath and shower — working condition, sealant condition, any cracks or chips
- Toilet — flushing mechanism, seat condition, cistern
- Basin and taps — working condition, drainage, any cracks
- Tiling and grouting — condition, any missing or cracked tiles
- Extractor fan — working condition
- Mirrors and cabinets — condition, fixings secure

### Exterior and outdoor areas

If the property has a garden, patio, balcony, parking space, or other outdoor area:

- Garden condition — lawn state, borders, fencing, gates
- Patio or decking — condition, any loose or damaged areas
- External walls — condition, any cracks or damage
- Guttering and drainage — visible condition
- Bin storage area — cleanliness, which bins belong to the property
- Shed or outbuilding — condition, contents, lock

## Photography Best Practices

Photographs transform your written notes from assertions into evidence. But poor photographs can actually weaken your position by suggesting you were not thorough or professional.

### Lighting

Natural daylight produces the most accurate photographs. Open curtains and blinds fully. Avoid using flash, which creates harsh shadows, washes out colours, and can hide or exaggerate marks. If a room is dark, turn on all lights and use a slow, steady hand rather than flash.

Photograph at the same time of day if possible — this gives consistent lighting across the whole report.

### Angles and composition

For each room, take:

1. **Wide-angle overview shots** from each corner, capturing the whole room. These establish context and show the overall condition
2. **Mid-range shots** of each wall, the floor, and the ceiling
3. **Close-up detail shots** of any specific marks, damage, or items of note

For close-ups, include a reference object for scale — a coin, ruler, or pen placed next to the mark or damage. This prevents disputes about the size or severity of issues.

### Timestamps and metadata

Use a camera or phone that automatically embeds date and time in photo metadata (EXIF data). Do not edit, crop, or filter photographs — any modification can be used to challenge their authenticity. Adjudicators and courts place significant weight on unedited, timestamped photographs.

If your camera does not timestamp automatically, hold a piece of paper showing the date and property address in the first photo of each room.

### What to photograph

Photograph **everything**, not just problem areas. If you only photograph damage, a tenant can argue that you selectively documented issues while ignoring the overall good condition. Comprehensive photography of pristine areas is just as important as documenting existing marks.

At minimum, photograph:
- Every room from multiple angles
- Every window, door, and their frames
- Every appliance (open and closed)
- All meter readings
- All keys laid out and counted
- Every existing mark, stain, scratch, or area of damage
- Exterior areas from multiple positions
- Any furnished items individually

## Condition Grading

Use a consistent grading system for every item. This creates an objective, comparable record. A common approach:

- **New/Excellent** — Unused or as-new condition, no marks or wear
- **Good** — Minor signs of use, no damage, fully functional
- **Fair** — Visible wear consistent with age, minor marks, fully functional
- **Poor** — Significant wear, damage, or issues requiring attention
- **Damaged** — Specific damage noted, beyond normal wear

Apply the same grading scale throughout the entire report. Inconsistent grading undermines credibility. If you grade a slightly scuffed wall as "good" in the hallway but "fair" in the bedroom, adjudicators will question your objectivity.

## How to Handle Disagreements with Tenants

Tenants have every right to challenge your assessment during the move-in inspection. In fact, their involvement strengthens the report. Here is how to handle disagreements constructively:

**Encourage participation.** Invite tenants to walk through the property with you and raise any concerns. A report that both parties contributed to carries far more weight than a landlord-only assessment.

**Document disagreements transparently.** If the tenant believes a mark or condition is worse than you have recorded, add their comment to the report alongside your own assessment. Do not suppress or ignore tenant observations — this will damage your credibility later.

**Take additional photographs.** If the tenant points out something you missed, photograph it immediately and add it to the report. This demonstrates good faith.

**Allow time for review.** Give the tenant a reasonable period (typically 7 days) to review the completed report and submit additional comments. Make clear in writing that the report will be finalised after this period.

**Both parties sign.** The completed report should be signed and dated by both the landlord and the tenant. Digital signatures with timestamps are acceptable and increasingly preferred by adjudicators.

## Digital vs Paper Reports

Paper reports — handwritten notes on printed forms — are still used by some landlords, but they have significant weaknesses:

- Handwriting can be illegible or ambiguous
- Paper forms get lost, damaged, or faded
- Photographs printed on paper lose quality
- No automatic timestamps or metadata
- Difficult to share or duplicate accurately
- Harder to present in dispute proceedings

Digital reports solve all of these problems. They produce clear, consistent, searchable records with embedded photographs, automatic timestamps, and easy sharing. Most deposit protection schemes now expect digital evidence as standard.

## How the Move-In Report Links to Check-Out

Your move-in report only has value if you create a corresponding check-out (move-out) report at the end of the tenancy. The two reports form a pair: the move-in shows what condition the property was in when the tenant took possession, and the check-out shows what condition it was in when they left.

Deposit deductions are justified by comparing the two. Any deterioration beyond fair wear and tear that is documented in the check-out but not present in the move-in report is attributable to the tenancy.

For this comparison to work, both reports must:
- Cover the same rooms, fixtures, and items
- Use the same condition grading system
- Include photographs of the same areas from the same angles
- Be signed by both parties where possible

This is why your move-in report structure matters so much — it sets the template for the check-out.

## Common Mistakes That Cost Landlords

**Rushing the inspection.** A proper move-in inspection takes 2-3 hours for a standard property. Landlords who try to complete it in 30 minutes miss critical details that become expensive gaps later.

**Not testing appliances.** Assuming an oven works because it looks clean is a common and costly error. Test every appliance while the tenant is present. If something fails during the tenancy and there is no record of it working at move-in, you may struggle to hold the tenant responsible.

**Photographing only problem areas.** If your report only contains photos of existing damage, you have no evidence of the areas that were in good condition. Photograph everything.

**Using vague language.** "Walls in OK condition" tells an adjudicator nothing. "Walls painted magnolia, good condition, two small pin holes on east wall above where picture hook was fitted" tells them everything they need to know.

**Not getting tenant sign-off.** A report signed only by the landlord is a statement of your opinion. A report signed by both parties is an agreed record of fact. The difference in evidential weight is significant.

**Failing to distribute the report.** The tenant must receive a copy of the completed report. If they cannot produce their copy in a dispute, and you cannot prove you sent it to them, adjudicators may question whether the report was created contemporaneously or assembled after the fact.

**Not keeping the report safe.** Store digital copies in at least two locations. If you lose the report, you lose your evidence — regardless of how thorough it was.

## Building a Complete Evidence Trail

The move-in inspection report is the foundation of your evidence trail, but it should not stand alone. Throughout the tenancy, maintain:

- **Periodic inspection reports** (at least every 6 months) showing ongoing property condition
- **Maintenance records** documenting any repairs and their outcomes
- **Correspondence** with the tenant about property condition
- **Photographs** from any mid-tenancy inspections
- **The check-out report** at the end

Together, these create a chronological evidence trail that shows the property's condition from start to finish. This is the standard that deposit protection schemes and courts expect. A single move-in report in isolation is good; a complete evidence trail is far stronger.

## Conclusion

A thorough move-in inspection report is your most valuable protection as a landlord. It takes time and effort to do properly, but that investment pays for itself many times over when disputes arise. Document every room, photograph everything, use consistent grading, get tenant sign-off, and keep the report safe.

The landlords who lose deposit disputes are almost never wrong about the damage — they simply cannot prove their case. A comprehensive move-in inspection report, created systematically and agreed by both parties, ensures you are never in that position.
